Concept

Degrees of
Freedom

DoF = the number of independent ways a robot can move. Every direction that's locked is a missing DoF.

```
Tank Drive — 2 DoF
Forward/backward + rotation. Simple, robust, great pushing power.
Mecanum — 3 DoF
Forward/backward + rotation + strafe sideways. Complex but highly maneuverable.
Human Arm — 7 DoF
Shoulder (3) + elbow (1) + wrist (3). That's why arms are so flexible.
Interactive

Center of
Gravity

✓ Low CoG

Battery and heavy parts mounted low on the chassis — below or at wheel axle height.

● STABLE
✗ High CoG

Heavy components stacked on top — tall, top-heavy robot is easily tipped by opponents or field obstacles.

● UNSTABLE
Build Rule Mount your REV Control Hub and battery as LOW as possible on the frame. Target: CoG below the wheel axle center.
```
F

Force

A push or pull in a straight line. Motors on your robot generate force to move it across the field.

F = m × a

m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s²)

τ

Torque

Rotational force. A motor's torque is what turns the wheel axle. Gear ratios amplify it at the cost of speed.

τ = F × r

r = lever arm length (m)

W

Work & Energy

Work = force applied over a distance. Your battery stores energy; motors convert it to mechanical work.

W = F × d

d = distance traveled (m)

Hardware 101:
Fasteners

A stripped bolt at a tournament costs you 20 minutes. Knowing your fasteners cold prevents that. Click each type to learn when and why you use it.

M4 Socket Head Bolt

✓ PRIMARY FASTENER

The M4 bolt is your workhorse. "M4" means metric, 4mm shaft diameter. It has a precisely-machined machine thread that requires a matching nut, threaded insert, or tapped hole on the other side. You'll use these on 90% of your GoBILDA or REV assembly.

Diameter
4mm (0.157")
Thread Pitch
0.7mm standard
Drive Type
Hex socket (3mm key)
Common Lengths
8, 10, 12, 16, 20mm
✦ Always pair with a matching M4 nut, Nylock nut, or threaded standoff. Never force it into an unthreaded hole — you'll strip the channel.

Self-Tapping Screw

⚠ PLASTIC / WOOD ONLY

Self-tappers have a sharp, aggressive thread that cuts its own path into soft material as you drive it. There's no need for a pre-threaded hole — it makes the thread itself. The tapered shaft and steep thread angle are the telltale visual signs.

Drive Type
Phillips / Torx
Material
Plastic, wood, thin sheet
Tip
Sharp / self-threading
FTC Use
Encoder brackets, covers
✗ Never use self-tappers in aluminum channel. They will rip through the soft metal, leaving a useless stripped hole that's very hard to repair.

Nylock Nut

✓ USE ON ALL MOVING JOINTS

A Nylock nut has a nylon insert at the top of the threading. As you drive the bolt in, the nylon grips the bolt thread and creates friction — enough to resist loosening from vibration. Regular hex nuts will back out after a few seconds of motor vibration. Nylocks won't.

Also Called
Lock nut, nylon insert nut
How to ID
Orange/white insert at top
Reusable?
Yes, but replace if worn
Alternative
Loctite Blue thread-lock
✦ Rule: any bolt that connects to a mechanism that moves — motor mounts, arm pivots, wheel hubs — gets a Nylock. Save regular hex nuts for static structural connections.
```

Standoff / Spacer

📐 PRECISION SPACING

A standoff is a hollow hex cylinder threaded on both ends (or both through-holes). It maintains a precise, fixed distance between two parts — like a frame rail and a circuit board, or two layers of a mechanism. Spacers are similar but solid and usually not threaded.

Common Lengths
6, 8, 10, 12, 16mm
Material
Aluminum or nylon
Thread Type
Female M4 both ends
FTC Use
REV hub mounting, boards
✦ Use aluminum standoffs for structural spacing; nylon standoffs near electronics to avoid electrical shorts. Stack multiple standoffs when you need custom lengths.

Hex Key (Allen Key)

🔑 HOW TO USE IT RIGHT

The hex key fits into the socket head of M4 bolts. It comes in an L-shape — one long arm and one short. Knowing which end to use matters a lot: the long end gives you reach into tight spaces; the short end gives you extra torque — but also extra risk of stripping.

Size for M4
3mm hex key
Material
Chrome vanadium steel
Kit Standard
Comes with GoBILDA/REV
Tip
Ball-end fits angled bolts
✦ Long end into the bolt — use for most work. You get reach into tight areas and less mechanical advantage (harder to overtighten).
✗ Short end = strip risk. Only use the short end for final tightening where you need the extra torque — never for the full fastening process.

Hands-On — 90 Minutes

Build Sequence

Follow these steps in order. Click a step to expand details. Mark it done with the green button — you'll see your progress build across the timeline.

```
1
⏱ 5 min
Gather Materials
Lay everything flat on the table before touching a bolt. A missing part mid-build costs time.
  • 4× aluminum channel pieces (12" each) — GoBILDA or REV
  • 4× 90° corner gussets
  • 16× M4 socket head bolts (8mm length)
  • 16× M4 Nylock nuts
  • 2× hex keys (2.5mm and 3mm)
  • 1× tape measure
  • Flat work surface (avoid carpet — it compresses)
2
⏱ 20 min
Assemble the Corners
Connect two channel pieces at 90° using a corner gusset. Snug — but don't fully tighten yet.
  • Hold two channel pieces at a right angle on the flat surface
  • Place the corner gusset over the joint — holes should align
  • Insert M4 bolts through both the gusset and channel holes
  • Thread Nylock nuts by hand until snug — stop when you feel resistance
  • Repeat for all 4 corners — you'll have a closed square when done
  • Leave a 1/4 turn of play in each bolt — you need to adjust in step 4
3
⏱ 15 min
Close the Frame
Connect all four sides into a closed rectangle. All corners should look 90°. Don't tighten yet.
  • Connect remaining corners one at a time
  • The last corner is the trickiest — flex the frame slightly if needed
  • Do a quick visual check: does it look like a square from above?
  • All bolts should be hand-tight with a tiny bit of play remaining
4
⏱ 10 min
The Square Test
Measure both diagonals. They must match within 1/16" (1.5mm). If not — loosen and shift.
  • Measure diagonal A: hook tape on inner hole of corner 1, pull to inner hole of corner 3 (opposite)
  • Note the measurement. Measure diagonal B: corner 2 to corner 4
  • Compare: if they match within 1/16", you're square — move to step 5
  • If they don't match: loosen all corner bolts by 1/4 turn, push the frame toward the longer diagonal, re-measure
  • Repeat until both diagonals match — this may take 2–3 attempts
5
⏱ 10 min
Tighten & Lock Down
Once square: fully tighten all bolts in a corner-to-corner pattern. Verify Nylocks are snug.
  • Use the long end of the hex key for most of the tightening
  • Tighten in order: corner 1 → corner 3 (opposite) → corner 2 → corner 4
  • Cross-pattern prevents warping — same as tightening wheel lug nuts
  • Final check: try to spin each Nylock nut by hand — it should not move
  • Re-measure diagonals one final time after tightening
6
⏱ 5 min
The Drop Test
Hold the frame 12" above the floor and release flat. Pass = no bolts moved, no warping.
  • Hold the frame horizontally at exactly 12" (use the tape measure to check)
  • Release — don't throw. Let gravity do the work
  • Pick it up immediately and check every corner with your hands
  • Try to wiggle each gusset: pass = no movement felt
  • Re-measure diagonals: must still be equal within 1/16"
  • If anything moved: identify the weak corner, tighten, and re-test
```

Reference

Vocabulary

Print this page or bookmark it. You'll hear these terms constantly. Know them cold by Week 3.

Extrusion
Aluminum channel cut in long lengths — the backbone of your robot frame. The consistent hole pattern lets you attach anything anywhere.
Gusset / Bracket
Corner plate that connects two pieces of channel at an angle, usually 90°. Provides rigidity at the joint.
M4 Bolt
Machine bolt, 4mm shaft diameter — the standard fastener for GoBILDA and REV kits. Needs a matching threaded hole or nut.
Nylock Nut
Hex nut with a nylon insert that resists loosening from vibration. Use on all moving joints and anywhere motors create vibration.
Torque
Rotational force = Force × lever arm length (N·m). Motors produce torque; gear ratios amplify it at the cost of speed.
Degrees of Freedom
Number of independent axes a robot can move in. Tank drive = 2 DoF. Mecanum = 3 DoF. More DoF = more maneuverable.
Center of Gravity
The single point where the robot's total weight effectively acts. Keep it as low as possible to resist tipping.
Diagonal Test
Measure both frame diagonals — if they are equal (within 1/16"), the frame is square. If not, loosen and adjust.
Drop Test
Drop the frame 12" flat to verify structural integrity under impact. Pass = no bolts moved, diagonals still equal.
Thread-Lock
Liquid adhesive (Loctite Blue) applied to bolt threads in high-vibration areas. Holds tight but can still be removed with tools.
Standoff
Hollow hex spacer threaded on both ends that maintains a precise fixed distance between two parts.
Pass-Through Hole
An un-threaded hole — the bolt passes all the way through and a nut goes on the other side. Distinct from a tapped/threaded hole.
Self-Tapping Screw
Screw with a sharp tip that cuts its own thread as it drives in. Only for plastic or wood — never aluminum channel.
GoBILDA Pattern
Hole spacing of 8mm center-to-center, in groups of 4 then a gap. All GoBILDA parts share this pattern for universal compatibility.
Machine Thread
Precisely-cut thread on bolts and nuts that requires a matching thread to engage. Unlike self-tapping which cuts its own path.
Hex Key (Allen Key)
L-shaped tool that fits into socket head bolts. For M4 bolts, you need a 3mm hex key. Long end = reach; short end = torque.
